<CaseCard label="Overview" index="01">

Bubble Buddy is a 2D action game where you play as a bubble fighting through hordes of underwater enemies. The core hook: you are the weapon. Firing parts of yourself is how you survive, so every shot is also a cost. That single trade-off ties size, speed, and health together into one decision the player keeps making.

<CaseCard label="My Role" index="02">

As Game Designer, I designed the core gameplay loop, enemy behaviors, and risk-reward systems that defined the game's identity. Working inside a strict 32-hour window, I focused on a simple mechanic that players could understand immediately but still have room to master.

- Designed the core size/speed/health trade-off, where bubble integrity sets the player's mass, movement speed, and survivability at the same time.
- Designed the "shoot yourself" weapon mechanic, where firing depletes health but is the only way to keep progressing.
- Designed enemy archetypes with distinct behavioral patterns so encounters read differently depending on the mix.
- Balanced bubble drop rates, enemy spawn patterns, and difficulty scaling.
- Created game design documentation and internal materials so the team could align on mechanics and systems under jam pressure.
- Playtested and iterated on the core mechanics throughout the jam timeline.

</CaseCard>

<CaseCard label="Reflection" index="03">

Bubble Buddy taught me how to design subtractively. To lean on emergent design and do more with less under extreme time pressure. With only 32 hours for the initial build, every system had to justify its existence and directly serve the core loop. The size/speed/health triangle became a proof-of-concept for how a single interconnected system can create depth without piling on complexity.

Designing across two jam themes also pushed me to make mechanics thematically cohesive. Turning "you are the weapon" into a literal survival cost (shooting yourself) made the bubble theme feel meaningful rather than decorative.

I am proud of how much emergent strategy came out of such a small foundation, and the team rallied to extend and polish the game past the initial jam deadline.

</CaseCard>
