<CaseCard label="Overview" index="01">

Stick It is a short stealth-action game set in the rural town of Greenridge, where the player character — a farmer named Pedro — sneaks through enemy territory to recover food stolen by a local agricultural cartel. The game draws visual tone from broad cartoon satire while taking its narrative cues from real-world reporting on agricultural corruption.

The project was built in a seven-day development sprint by a four-person team at Knite Studios. I led the team as project manager and owned design across the stealth loop, enemy AI, progression, and UI.

<CaseCard label="Design Goals" index="02">

Three constraints shaped every decision on the project:

1. The core loop had to be immediately readable. A player picking up the game for the first time should understand the rules of stealth within the first thirty seconds of play.
2. The short runtime meant every level needed to feel distinct without relying on new mechanics. Levels had to remix a small set of tools, not keep introducing them.
3. With seven days of build time, production risk had to be front-loaded. Systems were scoped and prototyped before art and level production began.

</CaseCard>

<CaseCard label="My Contributions" index="03">

As project manager, I coordinated the art, programming, design, and UI disciplines under a hard deadline. This included milestone planning, scope negotiation, and daily unblocking of cross-discipline dependencies.

As lead designer, I was responsible for:

- The core stealth loop — detection cones, noise propagation, distraction, and cover rules — tuned until getting spotted felt readable and fair.
- Guard AI behaviour across patrol, suspicion, and pursuit states, with recovery timings sized so the player could always trace the cause of a detection.
- Progression design, where each level introduced one additional tool or constraint against an otherwise stable rule set.
- UI and HUD design, including the visual grammar for detection state, inventory, and objective feedback.

I worked closely with the programmer throughout implementation to ensure design intent translated into functional mechanics, and to catch integration issues while they were still cheap to fix.

</CaseCard>

<CaseCard label="Outcome" index="04">

The game shipped on time on itch.io and was playable end-to-end by the jam deadline. The project reinforced a set of practical lessons about shipping under constraint — most importantly, that a single tightly-tuned core loop will carry a short game further than a broader set of half-finished systems.

Stick It was my first experience running a full cross-discipline team under jam-scale pressure, and the process informed how I approach scope, prototyping order, and cross-discipline communication on subsequent projects.

</CaseCard>
